Calculus in more detail

Introduction

As I said before, Calculus is the mathematical study of continuous change. But what exactly does that mean?

Well, imagine you have a car travelling at 50km/h. Calculus helps us understand and calculate these changes. How do we do this though? That's where derivatives and integrals come in!

Derivatives and Integrals: The Building Blocks of Calculus

Derivatives and integrals are the two fundamental concepts in calculus. They are like the building blocks that allow us to understand and analyze how things change.

A derivative represents the rate of change of a function at a specific point. It tells us how fast something is changing at that moment. For example, if we have a function that describes the position of a car over time, the derivative would give us the velocity of the car at any given time.

On the other hand, an integral represents the accumulation of a quantity over an interval. It allows us to calculate the total change or the area under a curve. For instance, if we have a function that describes the velocity of a car over time, the integral would give us the total distance traveled by the car over that time period.

Both derivatives and integrals are essential tools in calculus that help us understand and analyze the behavior of functions and the changes they undergo. They are used in various fields such as physics, engineering, economics, and many more to solve real-world problems involving rates of change and accumulation.

But how do we use derivatives and integrals in practice? Let's first start off with the basics of derivatives and integrals, before applying it into real-life problem solving scenarios.

Differentiation, Integration and Limits: How They Work In Calculus

Differentiation is the process of finding the derivative of a function. It involves applying specific rules and techniques to calculate the rate of change at any given point. For example, if we have a function that describes the position of a car over time, we can differentiate it to find the velocity of the car at any moment.

Integration, on the other hand, is the process of finding the integral of a function. It involves applying specific rules and techniques to calculate the accumulation of a quantity over an interval. For instance, if we have a function that describes the velocity of a car over time, we can integrate it to find the total distance traveled by the car over that time period.

Limits are another important concept in calculus. They help us understand the behavior of functions as they approach specific values or points. For example, if we have a function that describes the position of a car over time, we can use limits to analyze how the car's position changes as time approaches a certain value.

Here is an example of how to solve differentiation and integration problems in practice:

Example 1: Find the derivative of f(x) = 3x^2 + 2x + 1.

The first thing you need to know is the power rule for differentiation: d/dx[x^n] = nx^(n-1).

Example 2: Find the integral of g(x) = 4x^3.

The first thing you need to know is the power rule for integration: ∫x^n dx = (x^(n+1)) / (n+1) + C.

Example 3: Find the limit of h(x) = (2x^2 + 3x + 1)/(x^2 - 1) as x approaches 1.

Now let's solve example 1, step by step. So, the first we are going to do is we are going to multiply the 3 by 2, and then subtract 1 from the exponent of the first term.

So, we get 6x^(2-1) = 6x. Then, we are going to multiply the 2 by 1, and then subtract 1 from the exponent of the second term. So, we get 2. Finally, we are going to add the derivatives of each term together to get our final answer: f'(x) = 6x + 2.

Now let's solve example 2, step by step. So, the first thing we are going to do is we are going to add 1 to the exponent of the term, and then divide the term by the new exponent. So, we get (4x^(3+1)) / (3+1) + C = (4x^4)/4 + C = x^4 + C.

Finally let's solve example 3, before you have a turn at solving it yourself. So, the first thing we are going to do is we are going to factor the numerator and denominator. So, we get (2x^2 + 3x + 1) / (x^2 - 1) = (2x + 1)(x + 1)/(x - 1)(x + 1).

Then, we are going to cancel out the common factor of (x + 1) from the numerator and denominator. So, we get (2x + 1)/(x - 1). Finally, we are going to substitute x = 1 into the simplified expression to find the limit: (2(1) + 1)/(1 - 1) = 3/0. Since division by zero is undefined, we can conclude that the limit does not exist.
